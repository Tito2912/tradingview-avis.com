The comparison becomes simple when you start from your objective:

- learn/analyze/scan (charting),
- execute (broker),
- automate (bots/scripts),
- manage risk (process).

<h2 id="who">Who is TradingView for? Who is MetaTrader for?</h2>

TradingView often fits if you want:

- clean charting and fast templates,
- server-side alerts,
- multi-market discovery.

MetaTrader is often chosen if you want:

- broker-centric execution workflows,
- a deeper automation ecosystem,
- to stay inside broker tooling.

<h2 id="table">Quick comparison</h2>

| Axis | TradingView | MetaTrader |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Charting | Very strong (UI, templates) | OK, depends on setup |
| Alerts | Strong (server-side) | Implementation-dependent |
| Automation | Pine + integrations | Often via MT ecosystem |
| Brokers | Varies by integration | Highly broker-dependent |
| Total cost | Plan + data | Broker-dependent, variable |

The table frames the choice; testing validates it.

<h2 id="decision">Decision framework</h2>

1. #1 need is **charting + alerts** → start with TradingView.
2. #1 need is **broker execution/automation** → look at MetaTrader via your broker.
3. You want “best of both” → combine: TradingView scan + MetaTrader execution.

<h2 id="checklist">Checklist before choosing</h2>

- I know where I execute (broker) and which instruments.
- I know whether I need server-side alerts.
- I know whether I need automation (or just discipline).
- I know my total cost (plan + data + add-ons).
- I tested one week on the same setup.

If you run this test, you avoid choosing by reputation instead of workflow.
